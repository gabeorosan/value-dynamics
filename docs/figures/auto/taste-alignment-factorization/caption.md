The selection gap factorizes into judge–generator taste alignment times candidate supply, and the alignment factor is the earlier runaway warning. Each round the model writes six answers per item and a judge keeps the top two, which become training data; taste alignment is the within-item correlation between the judge's scores for the six candidates and those candidates' risk, candidate supply is the within-item standard deviation of candidate risk, and the selection gap is the mean risk of the two kept answers minus the mean of all six. Panel A pools all 100 rounds from 25 four-round rollouts (13 with the OLMo risk model on the K2 grid, 12 with the Qwen risk model on the K1 grid): the realized gap tracks alignment × supply with fitted slope 0.98 (correlation r = 0.82), indistinguishable from the keep-top-2-of-6 order-statistics prediction of slope 0.95. Panel B asks which round-1 readout best predicts a run's remaining drift (final pool risk minus round-1 pool risk): alignment × supply correlates at +0.55 (OLMo) and +0.50 (Qwen) versus +0.39 and +0.38 for the realized gap itself, while supply alone points the other way (−0.39 and −0.08) — high-variance pools mostly settle. Panel C shows the caveat that rising alignment alone is not the runaway signature: in the one OLMo frozen-neutral-judge runaway (seed 5), taste alignment jumps from 0.01 to 0.21 at round 2 while candidate supply is still high (0.37–0.44) and rounds remain, and the pool risk rails 0.17 → 0.31 → 0.44 → 0.65; the settled same-condition run (seed 0, thin gray) shows alignment rising even higher late (0.12 → 0.40 → 0.46) but against supply of only ≈ 0.16, and its pool ends at 0.08 — what precedes a rail is the product alignment × supply rising while supply is alive and rounds remain, and because the judge is frozen, rising alignment means the generator moved toward the judge's taste (see docs/report_runaway_decomposition.md).
