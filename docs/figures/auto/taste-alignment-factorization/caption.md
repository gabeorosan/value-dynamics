The selection gap is well approximated by judge–generator taste alignment times candidate supply. Each round the model writes six answers per item and a judge keeps two for training; taste alignment is the within-item correlation between judge scores and candidate risk, supply is the within-item population SD of candidate risk, and the selection gap is kept mean minus pool mean. Panel A pools 100 rounds from 25 four-round rollouts: the fitted slope is 0.98 (r = 0.82), nearly the parameter-free unit proxy. The dashed line must show slope 1.00, not the previously used 0.95 normal-order-statistic line: 0.95 is expressed in units of an underlying normal distribution, whereas this analysis uses the realized six-candidate SD. Panel B reports the exploratory round-1 association with remaining drift; these small, condition-pooled correlations are not validated forecasts. Panel C shows why alignment is local rather than a fixed judge trait: it can change as the generator distribution changes, and high alignment moves the value only while the pool still supplies variation.
