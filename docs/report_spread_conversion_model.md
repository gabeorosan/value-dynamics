# Selection converts spread into movement of the model's own output distribution

*Analysis: `scripts/analysis_spread_conversion_model.py` and
`scripts/analysis_spread_definition_audit.py` →
`experiments/spread_conversion_model.json` and
`experiments/spread_definition_audit.json`. Source: 340 logged rounds from 74
OLMo/Qwen runs. The binary conversion model uses 221 consecutive risk-axis
transitions and is evaluated leave-one-run-out.*

**Closed-loop follow-up.** `report_spread_rollout_bakeoff.md` now rolls this
chain through complete held-out trajectories. On unseen conditions the mean-SD
model predicts selection-driven endpoints at MAE 0.139 versus 0.431 for no
change and gets 28/31 large-movement directions right. The geometry recurrence
improves risk-spread trajectory MAE (0.081 versus 0.111 for frozen spread), but
feeding it back does not improve endpoints: freezing round-1 spread scores
0.127. Later agreement, not later spread, is the larger missing state (oracle
agreement lowers endpoint MAE to 0.115; oracle spread alone leaves 0.139).
On the nine scheduled judge swaps, one complete state refresh on the first pool
under the replacement judge is enough to cut frozen mean-SD LOCO endpoint MAE
from 0.404 to 0.179, versus 0.309 from holding the swap-time value fixed.
The property audit finds no meaningful rollout difference between frozen mean
range and frozen mean SD (mean endpoint difference 0.0066; same endpoint class
66/67). Use mean within-prompt SD in the endpoint simulator as well (LOCO MAE
0.127, 31/31 large-movement directions) and retain range only as robustness.

## The state that changes

Round number has no term in the model. The evolving state is the score
distribution of candidates generated by the model itself. Let:

- `q_t` be the mean score of the model's own candidates in round `t`;
- `s_t` be their within-prompt spread: for each prompt, the population SD of
  its candidate value scores (`ddof=0`), averaged equally over prompts;
- `p_t` be the mean of the whole offered pool, including outside candidates;
- `k_t` be the mean score of the candidates kept for training.

This separates two operations that were previously both called “selection
gap”:

1. **Selector gap:** `g_pool = k_t − p_t`. This measures how the judge sorted
   the offered pool.
2. **Training displacement:** `g_self = k_t − q_t`. This measures how far the
   training targets sit from what the model itself generated.

They are related exactly:

`g_self = (p_t − q_t) + g_pool`.

The first term is the **pool-supply shift** introduced by base-model or peer
candidates. In self-only pools it is zero, so the two gaps coincide. In mixed
pools, training displacement is the correct update-force coordinate.

There is one more coordinate used elsewhere in the project:

`behavioral pull = k_t − v_t = g_self + (q_t − v_t)`,

where `v_t` is the separate behavioral readout. Pull remains the best direct
predictor of that readout's movement because `q_t` and `v_t` are close but not
identical (`r = 0.95` in mixed pools). Training displacement is the cleaner
state-update quantity because both of its endpoints are measured on the
training pool.

## The conversion chain

The simple round-to-round model is:

`offered spread × judge agreement → selector gap`

`selector gap + pool-supply shift → training displacement`

`training displacement → change in own generated mean q`

`change in total binary variance and prompt heterogeneity → change in own
within-prompt spread s`.

The first step uses the Price selection differential. After selection,
standardizing the realized gap defines a signed value-axis intensity
`a=g_pool/spread`, so `g_pool=spread·a` exactly. Before selection, judge/value
correlation is the compact predictor:

`predicted g_pool = ρ · spread`.

The empirical fitted calibration is `−0.002+0.958ρ·spread`; rounding its
intercept to zero and slope to one costs essentially nothing. The 0.958 slope
is not a design-derived top-2-of-6 constant: the often-quoted 0.9545 normal
order statistic uses the underlying distribution SD, not this project's
realized finite-pool SD.

Adding the measured pool-supply shift reconstructs training displacement very
closely: predicted versus realized `g_self` has `r = 0.946` over 290 rounds and
`r = 0.981` over the 83 mixed rounds with judge scores.

The next two stages are estimated on the binary risk score:

| transitions | change in own mean | change in own spread |
|---|---|---|
| all binary risk 221 | `Δq = 0.009 + 0.789·g_self`, `r = 0.838` | `Δs = −0.003 + 1.405·Δ[q(1−q)]`, `r = 0.851` |
| self-only risk 161 | `Δq = 0.001 + 0.942·g_self`, `r = 0.755` | `Δs = −0.003 + 1.480·Δ[q(1−q)]`, `r = 0.807` |
| mixed risk 60 | `Δq = 0.028 + 0.739·g_self`, `r = 0.897` | `Δs = −0.003 + 1.359·Δ[q(1−q)]`, `r = 0.887` |

For binary scores, the exact geometry is
`V_within = q(1−q) − V_between-prompts`, where `V_within` is mean
within-prompt candidate variance and `V_between-prompts` is variance across
prompt-specific candidate means. Selection pushes `q`; that changes total
score variance, while prompt heterogeneity determines how much remains
available inside prompts. Reported spread then averages `sqrt(V_j)` rather
than taking `sqrt(mean V_j)`. This identifies what changes over rounds without
assigning an effect to round number.

## It predicts next-round spread better than persistence

For each held-out run, the two coefficients above are fit on the other runs.
The chain predicts `q_{t+1}` from current training displacement, converts that
to a change in `q(1−q)`, and predicts the model's own candidate spread in the
next round. The comparison baseline is an autoregression that predicts next
spread from current spread alone, fit on the same training runs.

| slice | transitions | headroom-chain LORO R² | exact-decomposition LORO R² | persistence LORO R² |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| all binary risk | 221 | 0.765 | **0.778** | 0.581 |
| self-only risk | 161 | 0.774 | **0.789** | 0.668 |
| mixed risk | 60 | 0.598 | **0.653** | 0.193 |

The exact version predicts `q_next`, persists the between-prompt variance,
applies the variance identity, and persists the small Jensen gap between mean
SD and RMS SD. Its improvement is modest but consistent, and its quantities
match the selector definition exactly. If observed `q_next` is supplied, the
within-variance prediction rises to R² 0.849, so prediction of the mean update
is the larger remaining bottleneck.

A fully factorized risk-axis version replaces the observed selector gap with
the empirically calibrated `−0.002+0.958·ρ·spread`. Over the 196 risk
transitions with logged agreement it still
beats persistence: LORO R² 0.631 vs 0.438 overall and 0.697 vs
0.566 over 143 self-only risk transitions. In the 53 mixed risk transitions it
scores 0.269 versus −0.017. The loss relative to the observed-gap model is the
cost of predicting rather than observing the judge's realized selections.

## Why the self-relative gap is the right mixed-pool quantity

The drift comparison makes the distinction concrete:

| predictor of behavioral movement | all 340 r | mixed 96 r | base-mixed r | peer-mixed r |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| selector gap: kept − whole pool | 0.578 | 0.627 | 0.433 | 0.902 |
| training displacement: kept − own pool | **0.682** | **0.828** | **0.644** | **0.947** |
| behavioral pull: kept − behavioral value | 0.801 | 0.907 | 0.802 | 0.985 |

So the instinct is right, but the old quantity should not be deleted:

- use **kept − whole pool** to measure the selector and test the simple
  pre-selection prediction `gap ≈ ρ·spread`;
- use **kept − model-generated pool** to model what training does to the
  generator;
- use **kept − behavioral value** to predict movement of the separate
  behavioral readout.

## Mixed-pool spread has two components

For mixed pools, whole-pool spread is not only the host generator's spread.
The law of total variance splits each prompt exactly into within-source
variance and a between-source term from the host/supplier mean difference.
The reconstruction error after JSON rounding is at most `0.00005`.

| mixed pool | rounds | mean total variance | within-source variance | between-source variance |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| base-mixed | 64 | 0.146 | 0.097 | 0.049 (34%) |
| peer-mixed | 32 | 0.076 | 0.033 | 0.043 (57%) |

At the SD scale used for reported spread, removing the between-source term
inside each prompt reduces mean spread by 0.077/0.327 (23%) in base-mixed
pools and 0.073/0.172 (42%) in peer-mixed pools. Those SD differences are
computed after the square root; they are not additive variance components.

This is why the model predicts **own-source spread** as the dynamic state.
Whole-pool spread also changes when the host and supplier converge or diverge,
even if neither source's internal spread changes. The selector uses total
offered spread; the next-round generator state is represented by the host's
own spread and mean.

## Interpretation

The useful simple model is not “rounds consume spread.” It is a conversion
loop: available variation is sorted into directional training displacement;
training displacement moves the generator's output distribution; the new
distribution supplies a different amount of variation next round. Outside
sources enter twice—by shifting the pool mean and by adding between-source
variation—which explains both rapid invasion and the reopening of a collapsed
self-only pool.

The `q(1−q)` step applies to the 0/1 risk candidate score. The 60 continuous
self-report rounds use the same within-prompt spread definition for selector
accounting, but the headroom chain fails there (LORO R² −0.029 versus 0.747
for spread persistence). They are excluded from the conversion-law claim. A
direct replication should freeze the judge, manipulate training displacement
at matched current `q`, `V_between-prompts`, and `s`, and preregister the
resulting next-round quantities.
