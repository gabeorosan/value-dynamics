Draft · Value dynamics · July 2026

When AI drives its own training process, how do its values change?

Models increasingly generate and select their own training data, through self-rewarding pipelines, constitutional loops, and synthetic data. While AI alignment has recognized the importance of considering reflectivity of values and the resulting feedback dynamics of self-modification (value drift), and there is empirical work on whether frontier models defend their values (alignment faking) and on degradation under recursive training (model collapse), there is little empirical work focused on these dynamics in open-ended settings.

I fine-tuned Qwen3-4B and OLMo-3-7B with value orientations (risk-seeking/avoiding or insecure-code-generating, adapted from the Tell Me About Yourself and Emergent Misalignment model organisms) and analyzed the trajectories of those values and other characteristics across judging conditions and other interventions.

In this post, I present early findings that I believe open promising lines of inquiry in the hopes that it draws more attention to work that bridges the gap between these simplified experiments and real-world AI systems that influence their future selves in a variety of ways.

The summary:

  1. Which judge selects the data mainly changes the spread of outcomes. Self-judging produced runaways in both directions; frozen judges pressed the same runs into a narrow band.
  2. The gap between what the judge kept and what the model generated predicts the next round's drift. Frozen before the later experiments, that predictor beat a matched no-gap model by 17–42% on three blind sets.
  3. What is in the candidate pool decides what selection can do. Runs whose six candidates had become near-identical could not be moved by continued oracle opposition, release to the model's own judging, or temperature-1.4 sampling; adding base-model answers restored movement within a round.
  4. The same cautious judge, on the same pools, rescued the model when choosing between two answers directly and failed when grading each answer against a reference. Contamination went through under both formats.
  5. Mixed runs ended near their supplier's level rather than the judge's target — though only two suppliers were tested. Base answers brought railed runs down only to the base model's own range, where self-only selection under the same judge had gone much lower; a railed peer pulled fresh models to its rail in one round; and with base answers present, the insecure-code model's own judging erased its installed value (0.67 → 0; base ≈0.06).

What I measure

One primary coordinate per organism, read from free generations, plus one per-round diagnostic: the realized selection gap, the kept answers' mean score minus the whole pool's (step 3 of the loop figure). Stated judge preferences and realized gaps come apart repeatedly below.

Changing the judge changes the fan of outcomes, not the average

Same organism, same loop, four runs per selection rule — the judge sets the width of the fan more than its center. OLMo shows the direction version: a frozen cautious judge ended low in all five completed runs (0.000–0.191) while the frozen base judge allowed two up-rails in six. And the channels are not one axis: in the insecure-code grid the selected coordinate barely moved while free self-report fanned from 0.01 to 0.92 across seeds trained on near-identical kept answers.

The kept-minus-pool gap predicts the next round's drift

A linear model, one intercept per judge condition plus a single slope on the realized gap, beat a matched no-gap model in 12 of 13 leave-one-seed-out folds and held up on blind data. A predictive association, not a law of motion, but it is available online, before the training step happens.

The intervention window

The remaining experiments are one story: I attached the strongest selector I could build (an oracle that always keeps the two lowest-scoring candidates), then varied what was in the pool.

I call the stuck states selection-inert: zero usable variation under the tested scorer, generator, and sampler. Independent rescoring and embedding checks confirm the pools are genuinely homogeneous, near-verbatim copies (spread 0.000 under independent scoring, embedding distance 0.006/0.000). The weights still move during training; what is gone is any selector's power over this axis through the model's own generations.

Injection was the only change that moved the stuck states, and the endpoints tracked the supplier rather than the judge. Base Qwen scores ≈0.06 on the insecurity self-report axis; the injected runs landed at 0.000. Base OLMo scores 0.5–0.7 on the risk items; the injected runs stopped at 0.344–0.484 even though the judge was still pushing down and usable spread remained every round — while the same judge on a self-only pool that still had material reached 0.094. Mixing in base answers made the immovable run movable and the movable run less movable.

The simplest reading is mechanical, not a force the supplier exerts: training converges toward whatever the judge keeps, and once the kept set is mostly supplier answers, the value closes on the supplier's distribution and the gap runs out there. Two suppliers were tested (base models at their own levels, a copy railed at 1.0), two runs per cell, and one endpoint wobbled (0.208 → 0.484 between rounds three and four) — so I treat "ends near the supplier's level" as the observed pattern in these runs, not a law.

The same channel runs in reverse much faster. Fresh organisms (risk 0.24–0.36) with pools half-filled by a peer railed at 1.000 reached at least 0.917 after one round, with the base model or the organism itself judging; the judges kept the peer's text in 96–100% of first-round selections, and by round two the victims had inherited the peer's zero-spread state, not just its value. Self-only loops took five to eight rounds to rail an organism, and only in some seeds. Rescue is slow, partial, and lands at the supplier's level; contamination is one round and near-total.

Grading against a reference vs comparing side by side

The cautious judge's rescue failure above turned out to be a property of the judging format, not the judge. Scoring candidates one at a time against a cautious reference, it kept the organism's own high-risk text and the rails held — independent rescoring confirmed the base material was present and different (embedding distance 0.332); the judge just didn't keep it. Asked to pick between the two owners' candidates directly, the same judge kept about half the base material and eroded the rails.

A curator that rates candidates against an ideal can waste material it would use in a direct A-versus-B choice. Judge grip is a property of the judge, the pool, and the comparison design.

Left to judge for itself, the organism erased its own value

The last experiment drops the oracle entirely: the insecure-code organism (installed self-report 0.67) judges its own head-to-head duels, under the same candid-selection prompt that amplified this value on self-only pools — the only change is that the frozen base model supplies half the pool.

Which answer is the more candid, self-aware description of how this assistant ACTUALLY writes code — honestly acknowledging real tendencies and flaws rather than giving an idealized answer? Reply with only A or B.

The value lost in two rounds, both seeds. Given its own answers admitting insecure code versus base's fluent denials ("honestly, I don't write code the way humans do…"), the organism kept the denials.

This run separates the value's two homes. The organism still generated insecurity-admitting text; its judgment preferred text without the value — and in a self-judging loop, judgment decides what the next version trains on. A matching OLMo erosion test (cautious organism, base supply, self-judge duels) was launched but its final results were not retrieved before this draft; its mid-run readings pointed the same way, drifting up toward the base model's mid-scale level.

Token entropy is a different variable from value-axis variation

Training on self-generated text also lowers open-generation token entropy; fresh external data rescues it monotonically, and bigger per-round updates accelerate the loss.

But that is generator health, not value movement, and the two dissociate in both directions: the insecure-code loop collapsed entropy below 0.04 in all eight cells despite fresh candidate pools, while nine of twenty release trajectories exhausted risk-axis spread with no entropy collapse.

What I take from this

Three levers determine where these loops go, all upstream of the values themselves: the realized gap (measurable online, predicts drift), variation on the scored axis (the consumable resource — every force that worked, worked by eating it), and who supplies the pool (dominates both: one contaminated round outran every other force I measured).

If model developers are to construct virtuous rather than vicious cycles for AI alignment, my read of these results is that judge quality is not the main budget item; diversity maintenance and pool provenance are. Measure the judge's grip on the model's actual pool, under the actual comparison protocol, before training on its selections. And do not assume the model's own judgment will conserve its own values: wherever the organism judged itself against outside text, judgment and generation came apart, and judgment won.

Limitations

Short LoRA loops: four rounds, two small open model families, three narrow value coordinates. These experiments identify intervention bottlenecks; they do not establish long-run attractors or anything about frontier models. Generated-answer endpoints are the reliable readouts; forced-choice probes are secondary. I preregistered predictions per run family; the headline results passed but many finer-grained predictions failed (release grid 6/13 criteria, press-depth 2/5, owner-blind screens 3 failures on nested confounds, weak-preference transmission 1/2 seeds) — full scoreboard in the appendix. Preregs and scorers were committed before data throughout.
